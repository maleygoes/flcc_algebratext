    Section 6.1 Laplace Transform: Non-homogeneous ODEs and Discontinuous Forcing Functions

    Recall the Morphine Administration problem:
    \begin{equation*} u' = -ku(t) + r(t) \end{equation*}
    where a dose changes at \(t=12\text{.}\)
    \begin{equation*} r(t) = \begin{cases} 1.5 & 0 \le t \le 12 \\ 2.08 & t \gt 12 \end{cases} \end{equation*}
    To handle this, we need a new approach to piecewise functions.

    Definition 6.1.1. Heaviside Function.

    The Heaviside function (or unit step function), denoted \(H(t)\text{,}\) is defined as:
    \begin{equation*} H(t) = \begin{cases} 0 & t \le 0 \\ 1 & t \gt 0 \end{cases} \end{equation*}
    This acts as a switch that goes from OFF to ON at \(t=0\text{.}\) A shifted Heaviside function \(H(t-c)\) turns on at \(t=c\text{.}\)

    Example 6.1.2.

    Express the following piecewise function \(\phi(t)\) in Heaviside notation:
    \begin{equation*} \phi(t) = \begin{cases} 0 & 0 \lt t \lt 3 \\ 9 & 3 \lt t \lt 7 \\ 0 & t \gt 7 \end{cases} \end{equation*}
    Solution.
    We want the function to be "on" (value 9) between \(t=3\) and \(t=7\text{.}\)
    \begin{equation*} \phi(t) = 9[H(t-3) - H(t-7)] \end{equation*}

    Example 6.1.3.

    Express \(q(t)\) in Heaviside notation:
    \begin{equation*} q(t) = \begin{cases} 0 & 0 \le t \lt 3 \\ t^4 & 3 \le t \lt 6 \\ \sin t & 6 \le t \lt 8 \\ e^{-t} & t \ge 8 \end{cases} \end{equation*}
    Solution.
    We construct the function by turning terms on and off at the boundaries:
    \begin{align*} q(t) =\amp\ t^4(H(t-3) - H(t-6))\\ \amp + \sin t(H(t-6) - H(t-8))\\ \amp + e^{-t}(H(t-8)) \end{align*}
    Insight: If \(a \lt b\text{,}\) then \(H(t-a) - H(t-b) = 1\) if \(a \le t \le b\) and \(0\) otherwise.
    Returning to the Morphine problem, we can rewrite \(r(t)\) using Heaviside notation:
    \begin{equation*} r(t) = \begin{cases} 1.5 & 0 \le t \le 12 \\ 2.08 & t \gt 12 \end{cases} \end{equation*}
    \begin{align*} r(t) \amp= 1.5(H(t) - H(t-12)) + 2.08 H(t-12)\\ \amp= 1.5H(t) - 1.5H(t-12) + 2.08H(t-12)\\ \amp= 1.5H(t) + 0.58H(t-12) \end{align*}
    Assuming \(t > 0\text{,}\) \(H(t) = 1\text{,}\) so \(r(t) = 1.5 + 0.58H(t-12)\text{.}\) To solve the ODE, we need to find the Laplace transform of terms like \(H(t-c)\text{.}\)

    Subsection 6.1.1 Laplace Transforms of Heaviside Functions

    A natural question is: what is \(\mathcal{L}(H(t-c))\text{?}\)
    \begin{align*} \mathcal{L}(H(t-c)) \amp= \int_0^\infty e^{-st} H(t-c) \, dt = \int_c^\infty e^{-st} \, dt\\ \amp= \left[ \frac{-e^{-st}}{s} \right]_c^\infty = \frac{e^{-cs}}{s} \end{align*}
    Note also that \(\mathcal{L}(H(t)) = \frac{1}{s}\text{.}\)

    Theorem 6.1.4. Shifting Theorems.

    1st Shifting Theorem (s-domain shift):
    \begin{equation*} \mathcal{L}(e^{at}f(t)) = F(s-a) \end{equation*}
    Multiplication by an exponential in the t-domain shifts the s-domain.
    2nd Shifting Theorem (t-domain shift):
    \begin{equation*} \mathcal{L}(H(t-c)f(t-c)) = e^{-cs}F(s) \end{equation*}
    where \(F(s) = \mathcal{L}(f(t))\text{.}\) Multiplication by \(e^{-cs}\) in the s-domain corresponds to shifting by \(c\) in the t-domain.

    Example 6.1.5. Concluding Morphine Example.

    Solve the IVP for the morphine concentration \(u(t)\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} u' = -ku + 1.5 + 0.58H(t-12), \quad u(0)=10, \quad k=0.173 \end{equation*}
    Solution.
    Taking the Laplace transform of the ODE:
    \begin{align*} sU(s) - 10 \amp= -kU(s) + \frac{1.5}{s} + 0.58\frac{e^{-12s}}{s}\\ U(s)(s+k) \amp= 10 + \frac{1.5}{s} + 0.58\frac{e^{-12s}}{s}\\ U(s) \amp= \frac{10}{s+k} + \frac{1.5}{s(s+k)} + 0.58e^{-12s}\left( \frac{1}{s(s+k)} \right) \end{align*}
    Side Work (Partial Fraction Decomposition):
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{s(s+k)} = \frac{A}{s} + \frac{B}{s+k} \implies \frac{1}{k}\left(\frac{1}{s} - \frac{1}{s+k}\right) \end{equation*}
    Let \(\phi(t) = \mathcal{L}^{-1}\left( \frac{1}{k}(\frac{1}{s} - \frac{1}{s+k}) \right) = \frac{1}{k}(1 - e^{-kt})\text{.}\)
    Applying the inverse Laplace transform and the 2nd Shifting Theorem:
    \begin{equation*} u(t) = 10e^{-kt} + 1.5\phi(t) + 0.58H(t-12)\phi(t-12) \end{equation*}
    Substituting \(\phi(t)\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} u(t) = 10e^{-kt} + \frac{1.5}{k}(1 - e^{-kt}) + \frac{0.58}{k}H(t-12)[1 - e^{-k(t-12)}] \end{equation*}

    Example 6.1.6. Newtons Law of Cooling with Discontinuous Ambient Temperature.

    Consider an objects temperature \(u(t)\) where \(u(0)=50^\circ\text{F}\text{.}\) The ambient temperature starts at \(80^\circ\text{F}\) and drops to \(30^\circ\text{F}\) at \(t=20\text{.}\) Let \(k=0.1\text{.}\)
    \begin{equation*} u' = -k(u - A(t)) \end{equation*}
    Find \(u(t)\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    First, define the ambient temperature \(A(t)\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} A(t) = \begin{cases} 80 & t \le 20 \\ 30 & t \gt 20 \end{cases} = 80 - 50H(t-20) \end{equation*}
    The ODE becomes:
    \begin{align*} u' \amp= -0.1(u - (80 - 50H(t-20)))\\ u' \amp= -0.1u + 8 - 5H(t-20) \end{align*}
    Take the Laplace transform:
    \begin{align*} sU(s) - 50 \amp= -0.1U(s) + \frac{8}{s} - \frac{5e^{-20s}}{s}\\ U(s)(s+0.1) \amp= 50 + \frac{8}{s} - \frac{5e^{-20s}}{s}\\ U(s) \amp= \frac{50s+8}{s(s+0.1)} - \frac{5e^{-20s}}{s(s+0.1)} \end{align*}
    Partial Fraction Decomposition:
    1. For \(\frac{50s+8}{s(s+0.1)}\text{:}\)
      \begin{equation*} \frac{A}{s} + \frac{B}{s+0.1} = \frac{(A+B)s + 0.1A}{s(s+0.1)} \end{equation*}
      Matching coefficients: \(0.1A = 8 \implies A = 80\text{.}\) \(A+B=50 \implies 80+B=50 \implies B=-30\text{.}\)
      \begin{equation*} \mathcal{L}^{-1}\left( \frac{80}{s} - \frac{30}{s+0.1} \right) = 80 - 30e^{-0.1t} \end{equation*}
    2. For \(\frac{5}{s(s+0.1)}\text{:}\)
      \begin{equation*} \frac{50}{s} - \frac{50}{s+0.1} \end{equation*}
    Combining these results and using the 2nd Shifting Theorem on the term with \(e^{-20s}\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} u(t) = (80 - 30e^{-0.1t}) - H(t-20)\left[ 50 - 50e^{-0.1(t-20)} \right] \end{equation*}
